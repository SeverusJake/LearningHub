# 06 — POD Merch

**Model:** Original print-on-demand designs — text-based and graphic art — uploaded to Redbubble and TeePublic, who handle all printing, shipping, customer service, and returns on shirts, stickers, mugs, and everything else in their catalog; you never touch inventory or fulfillment · **First $ (typical):** 3-8wks · **Ceiling:** $20-300/mo · **Payout:** PayPal

**AI does:** niche research, design briefs and text/layout concepts, and title/tag/description copy for every upload · **You do:** create both accounts, trademark-check every single phrase before it goes live, upload the designs, and review performance weekly

## Honest expectations

Redbubble and TeePublic are both well over a decade old and stuffed with millions of existing designs, so generic art — a wolf howling at the moon, a rainbow peace sign, "good vibes only" in a swirly font — is already covered a thousand times over and won't surface in on-platform search no matter how well it's drawn. Wins come from specific niches, not broad ones: a profession's inside joke (ER nurses, electricians, dog groomers), a hobby's specific in-crowd reference (pickleball, home coffee roasting, tabletop dungeon masters), or a narrow identity/pride angle — something a small, specific audience will type into search by name, not something everyone might vaguely like. For a beginner catalog, text-based designs (a pun, a slogan, a profession title in clean typography) reliably outsell complex illustrated graphics: they're faster to make, faster to trademark-check, and closer to what people actually type into POD search bars. This is a volume game, not a hits game — any single design's odds of a sale are low, so expect to need somewhere around 150-250+ designs live before a real pattern (a niche or format that's actually converting) emerges from the noise. Judge the catalog every few weeks, not any one design in its first two weeks.

## Rules and compliance

**This is the strictest folder in the track — read this in full before uploading a single design.**

- **Trademark-check every single phrase, every single time, before it goes live.** Run each candidate word or phrase through the USPTO's free TESS database (tmsearch.uspto.gov) for both an exact match and a phonetic/sound-alike search, and separately check it against Redbubble's and TeePublic's own current published IP and content policies — each platform maintains its own banned-terms guidance and brand-protection process, and a clean TESS result does not automatically mean a platform will allow it. This applies to every phrase on every design, not just the ones that "feel" risky.
- **Zero fan-art. Zero brand names, logos, or slogans you don't own. Zero celebrity names, likenesses, or catchphrases.** This includes parody and "inspired by" tributes — POD platforms treat unofficial fan content the same as direct infringement, and both Redbubble and TeePublic will permanently deactivate an account for repeated IP strikes, not just pull the one design. There is no "just this once" that's worth the account.
- **Original text and original graphics only.** Every word and every visual element has to be something you (or your AI-assisted process) actually created — no tracing, no lightly-edited stock art, no meme templates or fonts you don't hold the rights to use commercially.
- **Verify, don't assume, each platform's currently-published AI-art disclosure policy.** Both platforms publish rules about disclosing AI-generated or AI-assisted content, and those rules change over time. Read Redbubble's and TeePublic's own help-center/seller-policy pages yourself before your first upload, check again periodically, and disclose exactly as each one currently requires — treat this document's mention of the rule as a reminder to go verify it, not as the verification itself.

Full compliance floor (applies to every folder in this track, no exceptions):

- No fake reviews.
- No account misrepresentation or sharing.
- No spam.
- No undisclosed AI where a platform requires disclosure.
- No trademark/IP infringement.
- No fabricated credentials.
- Income figures are ranges with no guarantee.
- Taxes are the learner's own responsibility (this is not tax advice).

## Eligibility check

Do this before producing a single design — if payout isn't confirmed on both platforms, everything after this is wasted effort.

1. Create a Redbubble account (redbubble.com) as an artist/seller.
2. Create a TeePublic account (teepublic.com) as a seller.
3. In each platform's own payment/payout settings, confirm PayPal is actually listed and active as your payout method, and note each platform's current payout threshold and schedule (they differ, and both can change) directly from that platform's own payout page — do not assume last year's numbers still hold.
4. Only after both accounts exist and PayPal payout is confirmed live on each, move to Phase 1 of the playbook.

## Kill / scale criteria

**Kill this folder** if, after 12 weeks with 200+ designs live across both platforms combined, total revenue is under $10/mo. That's a real signal the catalog isn't finding buyers at any meaningful rate — not a reason to grind out more designs blindly. Bank whatever niche/format lessons you learned and put weekend time elsewhere.

**Scale a winning niche** the moment any single niche shows repeat sales (not one fluke sale) — build it out to 20 design variations (color-ways, alternate phrasings, different layout treatments of the same joke or angle) and start timing new uploads for that niche 6-8 weeks ahead of its seasonal peak, per the calendar in PLAYBOOK.md.
